The usual mistake is to treat old software as failed infrastructure. We ask
whether it still runs, whether it can compete, whether a modern replacement
exists. Those questions are practical, but they miss the point.

Software is also a record of taste. The constraint of a small screen, a slow
disk, a narrow memory budget, and an operating system with sharp edges forced
decisions into the open. A finished program had to choose a shape.

That shape is worth studying. It tells us what the author considered obvious,
what the user was expected to learn, and where the machine was allowed to show
itself.

Modern software often hides its material. Old software cannot. The seams are
visible, but the visibility is not always a flaw. Sometimes it is the whole
reason the object is beautiful.
